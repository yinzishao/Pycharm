<span class="focusParagraph"><p><span class="articleLocation">BEIJING</span> China will contribute $40 billion to set up a Silk Road infrastructure fund to boost connectivity across Asia, President Xi Jinping announced on Saturday, the latest Chinese project to spread the largesse of its own economic growth.</p></span><span id="midArticle_0"></span><p>China has dangled financial and trade incentives before, mostly to Central Asia but also to countries in South Asia, backing efforts to resurrect the old Silk Road trading route that once carried treasures between China and the Mediterranean.</p><span id="midArticle_1"></span><p>The fund will be for investing in infrastructure, resources and industrial and financial cooperation, among other projects, Xi said, according to Xinhua.</p><span id="midArticle_2"></span><p>The goal of the fund is to "break the connectivity bottleneck" in Asia, state media quoted Xi as saying during a meeting in Beijing with leaders from Bangladesh, Cambodia, Laos, Mongolia, Myanmar, Pakistan and Tajikistan.</p><span id="midArticle_3"></span><p>The Silk Road Fund will be "open" and welcome investors from Asia and beyond to "actively" take part in the project, Xi was cited as saying, ahead of a separate summit of the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) grouping, also being held in the Chinese capital.</p><span id="midArticle_4"></span><p>It was not immediately clear precisely how the fund would work, when it would start operations or where it would be based, though in all likelihood it would be China.</p><span id="midArticle_5"></span>

        <span class="third-article-divide"></span><p>One of the ways it has done this is to offer large loans to places like Southeast Asia and Africa, to show that China is a benign growing power only interested in helping others escape poverty in the way it has itself over the past three decades.</p><span id="midArticle_12"></span><p>Last month, Xi unveiled the $50 billion China-backed Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, seen as a challenge to the World Bank and Asian Development Bank, both multilateral lenders that count Washington and its allies as their biggest financial backers.</p><span id="midArticle_13"></span><p>China has sought to allay concerns that its new bank aims to undermine the World Bank and Asian Development Bank, saying it wants to learn from their experience and that there are more than enough projects around for all the lenders to fund.</p><span id="midArticle_14"></span><p></p><span id="midArticle_15"></span><p> (Reporting by Paul Carsten and <a href="http://blogs.reuters.com/search/journalist.php?edition=us&n=ben.blanchard&">Ben Blanchard</a>; Editing by <a href="http://blogs.reuters.com/search/journalist.php?edition=us&n=jeremy.laurence&">Jeremy Laurence</a> and <a href="http://blogs.reuters.com/search/journalist.php?edition=us&n=ian.geoghegan&">Ian Geoghegan</a>)</p><span id="midArticle_16"></span></span>

function weChatBox_show(tag,url){

if ( $('html').hasClass( 'canvas' ) ) {
	$("#shr-qrcode").qrcode({
		render: "canvas",
		width:178,
		height:178,
		text: url
	});
} else {
	$("#shr-qrcode").qrcode({
		render: "table",
		width:178,
		height:178,
		text: url
	});
}
    shrBoxShow(tag);
}
